Overview

is a Web-based application that allows you to manage one or more budgets. With it, you can do the following:

  1. Create any number of budgets. For most people, one budget is enough. It is often your own personal budget. If you live with another person, however, you may need more than one: a household budget (to track shared expenses, like utilities and food) and a personal budget for your own spending. In these cases, an allowance is given from the joint account to each personal account.

Keeping money separate between a household budget and several personal budgets is a well-known good practice for maintaining healthy relationships.

As long as the couple agrees on a joint/shared budget, each is free to spend within their own personal budget.

  1. Control access to other users. You may want to give access of your budget to another person. We allow you to grant users one of three levels of access, from ADMIN (full access) to MODIFY (limited editing) to VIEW (read-only access).
  2. By default, no one else has access to a budget but you.
  1. Record every transaction in your budget. A transaction is just like it sounds: any transfer of money into or out of your budget. This includes spending as well as income from paychecks and any other form of reimbursement or (monetary) gift.

To get the most value out of this tool, you should record every transaction. This takes a good amount of discipline and perseverence, but it allows you to have a holistic view of all of your spending.

Once you start, it can be an easy habit to maintain.

  1. Track how you paid for each transaction. A payment method is a way to purchase something. It may be a credit card, a bank account (which involves transfers of money, like Venmo) or cash.
  2. We can show all of the transactions within a payment method. This simulates a bank or credit card statement and allows you to check our record of transactions against what a bank says.
  3. Each transaction is associated with a single payment method.
  1. Put transactions into categories. A category is a way to group a set of transactions together. For example, you can create a category called Eating Out which records spending on restaurants. Later, you can monitor how much you are spending in this category and whether it is within the bounds that you've set for this budget.
  2. Each transaction is associated with a single category.
  1. Allocate money from your income into each category. You specify how much money of your income should go to each category. This is how a budget works: if you spend within the means that you establish, you will have enough funds for all of your goals.
  2. You can easily change the amount of money you allocate for a category, however, you must work within your available income.
  1. Two types of categories: normal and subaccount. The normal category is one in which you intend to spend all of its allotment during the course of a month. For example, if you have a category for Groceries, you typically expect to spend all of it each month. At the start of a new month, you are given a new allotment for more groceries. The balance of money you have at the end of the month goes into the slush fund. This includes a surplus (extra money) and a deficit (overspending).

The slush fund is a system-defined category that automatically receives surpluses (and deficits) from all of your normal categories at the end of the month. This can act as a safety measure for unexpected spending.

A well managed budget keeps your slush fund in the black (surplus). It can run a deficit, but you can also fund it directly, if necessary.

  1. A subaccount is a category where you have to save up money for more than one month. For example, you may have a bill that you owe every 6 months. Rather than incur a big charge every time this occurs, you save up 1/6 of the bill each month. When the bill comes, you have the funds saved to pay it in full. The balance at the end of the month is not added (or subtracted) from the slush fund; it remains in the category.

A well-designed budget anticipates irregular payments (i.e., ones that occur once or twice a year) and spreads the cost evenly over each month.

In this way, there is no stress when a bill becomes due.

  1. A Dashboard alerts you to the current state of your budget. For example, your dashboard can inform you of the following:
    • When a particular bill is due
    • The overall health of each category. Are you overspending (or near to overspending) on a category? You can check whether you are on track to end the month on budget.
    • How much money you can spend today to remain on track.
  2. Monitor historical trends and tune your budget. By using over time, you'll be able to establish better estimates for your spending habits. For example, you may believe that you only need $600 a month for groceries. However, by examining a year's worth of data, you'll know more precisely whether this estimate is accurate.

The best way to evolve your budget is to do end of year analyses. This is the practice of looking at how much money you spent in each category and adjusting your future budget to reflect more realistic goals.

For example, you may learn that your estimates where too aggressive and that meeting those spending goals was too difficult. That's okay as long as you adjust future spending by defunding some categories that are less important.

Money Flows

It is important to understand how money "flows" through your budget. The drawing below illustrates a budget with four categories (called Normal #1, Normal #2, Subacct #1 and Subacct #2).

  1. The INCOME category. This is a system-defined category where you associate transactions related to your source of income. budgete helps you define your monthly budget.
  2. Fund your categories. The INCOME category funds all of your categories. This is done automatically for you once you establish a budget amount for each category. You can see money flowing from INCOME category to each one you define.
  3. Associate each transaction with a category. Every time you record your spending, you associate it with a category. In this way, you are spending from your category.
  4. The balance of normal categories go back to the SLUSH category. Like the INCOME category, also creates a SLUSH category for you. At the end of the month, any unspent money goes to slush. If you happened to overspend in a given month, don’t worry--the SLUSH category will cover the difference.
  5. This makes sense for many categories like Groceries and many monthly utilities.
  6. Subaccounts maintain a balance over months. Sometimes, you need a category to track a future bill that occurs once a year. In this case, you don’t want the balance at the end of the month to go back to the SLUSH category, but remain inside it’s own category. This allows you to see how the funds accrue and enable you to pay that future bill without affecting your monthly spending.

Modes of operation

Before we get too deep into the mechanics of a budget, we should talk about the different ways that a user can interact with a budget. There are fundamentally three different modes of interacting with a budget:

  1. Defining and Configuring. This is the process of deciding what categories your budget should consist of and how much money you want to allocate to each. It is the process of defining your payment methods (identifying the bank account and credit cards that you want to manage). It is also the process of identifying how much money in your current bank account should go to paying the next credit card bills and funding your subaccounts.
  2. Typically, this is done once at the beginning, however, you can always re-configure your budget over time.
  3. Tracking and Verifying. This is the process of recording your transactions (a process I call "tracking") and verifying your budget against bank statements. Each statement may refer to a checking account, credit card or a store card.
  4. When we find new (unrecorded) transactions on one of these statements, we add them to our budget. This gives us a complete picture of your spending. produces its version of each bank statement and you compare it for correctness. In the end, the balances should be identical.
  5. We can verify spending for each payment method, whether it is a checking account, credit card or cash.
  6. This is your day-by-day process where you ensure that you are meeting your budget. It enables you to make good decisions, like Should we go out for dinner tonight? or Can I afford to purchase some new clothes?
  7. Forecasting and Right-sizing. This is done after you have several months of budget data and you forecast future needs. For example, you can look at your budget after six months and answer questions like, If I follow this budget, will I have enough money saved up for a downpayment on a car in a future month?
  8. Often at the end of the calendar year, I like to review how I did and correct my category allocations for the future year. Maybe I had overestimated how much money I would need for Auto Maintenance. Maybe there were too many unexpected costs associated with our medical coverage. Starting in January, I can have newly sized categories that reflect my current habits and situation.

assists you in each mode and places controls on who may perform these operations. For example, when you are in the mode of Defining and Configuring, you are essentially an Administrator. If you are working with another person on a shared budget, you may want to grant this user the same permissions, but that is up to you. If you are Tracking and Verifying, you are in the mode of a Editor. If you are doing Forecasting and Right-sizing, you are in the mode of a Viewer.

Finally, it is worth noting that permissions are inclusive of lower ones. That is, if you are an Editor, you also have the rights of the next lower level, which is a Viewer. It would be hard to be an Editor if you couldn't also view the data. Therefore, an Administrator has all of the permissions of an Editor and Viewer, so choose the permissions you grant wisely!

Getting Starting

Here’s how you begin the process of defining and configuring your first budget.

  1. Create a new Budget. Give your new budget a name (and an optional description). This is just a simple form and one click. Easy.
  2. Record your income and create your categories. There are two steps. First, record how much money you will have as income for this budget. We are looking for the net dollar amount. Second, we need to divide this income across a set of categories. Here, you create a category and tell us:
    • The name of the category.
    • Whether it is a normal category or a subaccount.
    • How much money you need. We can help you if your category is based on a yearly bill and you want to save up an equal portion each month.
  3. Your goal is to allocate all of your (net) income across all of the categories. The remainder will go to your Slush category.

  4. How do I know whether I should use a normal category or a subaccount? If you intend to spend all of the money in a category in the course of a month, it should be a normal category. Any overspending (or underspending) is resolved by the Slush category.

    If you need to save up over the course of one or more months, you should mark your category as a subaccount. In this case, any overspending (or underspending) is maintained in the category. Use this if you need to pay off a bill on a frequency of two months to a year.

    When you save for upcoming (big) bills over several months (using an equal amount each time), you ensure that you are not affected when the bill becomes due. This makes it easier to maintain your budget.

  5. Define all of the payment methods associated with this budget. There are three types of payment methods: those that you pay with a bank account (like a checking account), a credit (or store) card and cash. While we can support many credit (or store) cards, we can only support one bank account.

  6. At this time, we can only support one bank account because we need to match all of your spending against a single bank account.

    If you had another bank account which you wanted to use for purchases, we wouldn’t be able to produce a complete statement for it, and therefore you couldn’t reconcile it.

    If you live with another person and have separate bank accounts for personal and joint uses, then it is best to create two separate budgets; one for each use.

    In this way, it would satisfy the limitations of by requiring one checking account with each budget.

    Much like the checking account restriction, it is important to note that credit (and store) cards are reconciled against a budget. That means that we will try to match the balance of your credit card (and what the issuer says) with what our budget says. If they don’t match up, then we are urged to find the missing transactions and record them in our budget.
  7. If a credit (or store card) has purchase outside of this budget, then it becomes hard to account for that in our application.
  8. Allocate from your checking account to credit cards, subaccounts. Your checking account has money saved in it for several purposes. It may have money saved up to pay a future credit card bill. You may also want to apportion some of it to an upcoming payment to another institution.
  9. You can specify how much money you want to go to each credit card, and how much to give to each subaccount category. Recall that a subaccount category may have a bill in a few months. You can put aside money to each of these categories so that when the bill is due, you’ll have enough saved up.

  10. Start your new budget on the 1st of the month. In this way, you can easily determine how much you have to save up for credit card purchases in the prior months and can help you figure out how much money you need for each subaccount.

    For all transactions in this month, they should be recorded in your budget.
